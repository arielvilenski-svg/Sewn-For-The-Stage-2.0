THROUGH THE AGES

Renaissance

1400 – 1600

The Renaissance marked the beginning of fashion as a form of self-expression and social status. Particularly in Elizabethan England, costume design relied heavily on visual ornamentation, rich fabrics such as velvet and brocade, and vibrant colour to signify class and status. Characters of lower class were depicted in simpler fabrics with muted colours to visually convey stark contrast.

Victorian Burlesque

1850s

Victorian Burlesque rose in popularity for its exuberance and flamboyance in a time of heavy seclusion and censorship. Costumes typically included risqué pieces of corsets, stockings, feathers, and fans, designed to reveal and outline the female figure. Using sheer material such as lace and mesh, Victorian Burlesque costuming served as a satirical dig at societal expectations and norms.

Vaudeville

1870s – 1930s

Vaudeville costumes were theatrical, colourful, and highly diverse throughout a single production. Each costume was tailored to be specific to the act — ranging from elegant attire to overly exaggerated comedic pieces. Designed to be eye-catching from a distance through bold patterns, sparkles, and bright fabrics, they ensured visibility for large audiences.

19th Century

1900s – 2000s

The 19th century saw significant influence from the Romantic and Realist movements reflected within costumes. Paintings, sculptures, and books were heavily studied by costume designers to create more historically accurate and authentic looks that retained the richness of prior eras. This era began to blend accuracy in visuals with the creativity of costuming that maintains the joy of theatrics.

2000s

2000s – 2010s

With a rise in runway and celebrity fashion came new sources of inspiration for theatrical costume designers. Costumes were combining historical accuracy with worlds of fantasy and contemporary fashion trends more than ever, even bringing fashion designers into costuming teams for popular Broadway productions such as Hamilton and Wicked.

Present Day

2020s – Present

Modern day theatre costuming sees huge rises in blending innovation and creativity with ecological sustainability and technological integration. Pieces are now more minimalistic and derived from organic or recycled fabrics, while incorporation of LED lights, digital prints, and creative AI integration have evolved the visual storytelling of onstage looks in ways never before possible.
