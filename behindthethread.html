BEHIND THE THREAD

Materials

A material known for its luminosity, vibrance, and soft feel, silk is heavily used in costuming, particularly for characters of high class status or power to symbolize their status.

A lightweight and flowy fabric, designers often use chiffon for costumes requiring a delicate appearance and energy. The end result is a fluid, airy silhouette that provides lots of movement room and expression through interaction.

The fabric’s slight sheerness, continuous shine, and structured drapery have been key qualities that make organza a staple for pieces such as dance costumes, bridal looks, and fantasy garments for generations.

Made of ultra-fine netting and a hexagonal open structure, tulle is recognized for its ability to create volume without weight and to hold shape when layered or stiffened. The material as a result has been used by countless designers for pieces such as tutus, skirts, and wings, among others.

One of the most commonly used fabrics in modern day, polyester offers durability and strength unlike many competing materials that make it suitable across numerous types of clothing and costumes.

A must-have material in costume design, Nylon is famous for its lightweight feel, wrinkle resistance, and quick-drying attributes that make it a versatile and ideal material for theatrical pieces, especially when performing for countless hours under bright lights and through excessive movement.

Special & Practical Effects

To create the illusion of flight, such as in iconic performances like “Defying Gravity” in the broadway production of “Wicked”, what is most often used on stage is a kind or combination of wire system, counterweight system, harness, or pulley. Each method allows for smooth and controlled movements, while costumes and lighting serve as concealment for these machines.

Smoke machines are strategically placed around the costume and are programmed by tech staff to release smoke at particular timing and for specific durations.

An essential component of a production, sound design masterfully crafts soundscapes, conjures ideal elements such as wildlife or weather, evoke emotion or heightened tension within scenes, and are overall key to complementing visual parts of storytelling for the complete package in immersive experience. Through strategic placement of speakers on costumes, sound can be controlled to come from specific individuals or area which can further add precision to events within a production.

Quick Changes

Quick changes both on and off stage are meticulously planned to ensure perfect timing, avoidance of interruption, and a result of seamlessness that avoids a character/actor appearing messy or rushed.

Actors will often wear multiplayers of clothing when a quick change needs to be completed on stage, with the intended revealing costume hidden underneath items such as coats or capes.

Backstage assistance is crucial for quick changes, ensuring that costumes are properly managed and actors can transition smoothly between scenes.

When quick changes are able to be completed backstage or in the wings, a tremendous help in speed comes from a team of staff backstage that provide assistance to actors when changing, helping with removals and applying of multiple items at once.

For onstage quick changes, special effects such as lighting, smoke, or movement from other actors often serve as a distraction to the audience to keep them unfocused on possible changes onstage, to further create the magical illusion and help the process go unnoticed.

Character Symbolism

Colours in costuming can show evolution and change within emotion, status, or personal growth within a character. A modern day example is the colour evolution in “Hamilton” as Alexander starts as a poor man wearing a brown jacket and ends in his position as president wearing a rich dark green.

Vast change in style choices of a character can also symbolize changes made in a character’s journey. In “Wicked”, we see Elphaba enter in soft, academic style clothing, covering much of her physical features in an attempt to appear normal. As she transitions into the wicked witch, we see her embrace her silhouette in tighter fitting dresses made of sheerer and darker material, signifying her grief and loss of old self and in a sarcastic attempt to fit her society’s new expectations of her.

Layering of costumes on a character can also reflect the story being presented on stage and its meaning within character. “Hadestown”’s Eurydice starts off as a closed off character, represented by her hiding in heavy coats and excessive layers, her skin entirely covered with long sleeves and tights and tall boots. As she opens up and expresses her newfound vulnerability, she sheds her layers in exchange for more revelation of skin, allowing her more literal and physical connection with Orpheus whom she has grown closer to.

Accessories play just as important of a role in development of characters, as evidenced by plays such as “My Fair Lady” through characters such as Eliza Doolittle. Eliza starts her story in minimal clothing and accessories, signifying her current status and outlook on self presentation. As she undergoes societal transformation, we see her take more pride in her physical appearance, complete with newfound accessories of large and eccentric form that represent her new power and societal acceptance.
