ICONIC COSTUMES

Andrew Lloyd Webber - The Phantom of the Opera

Phantom - Black & White Tuxedo & Half-Mask

Designer: Maria Björnson

Costume designer Maria Björnson is accredited to one of the world's most notable broadway costumes of all time, sourced from the original 1910 novel of Phantom Of The Opera by Gaston Leroux. The phantom’s half-mask has served as a primary piece of broadway iconography for the past 30+ years, carefully disguising horror and brutality with an attempt at elegance and a clean, blank slate. Paired with a never-changing sleek black tuxedo to actively contrast the darkness and lightness within the Phantom, the look remains symbolically timeless and forever culturally remembered.

Lin-Manuel Miranda - Hamilton

Alexander Hamilton et. al - Revolutionary Wartime Era Blue Tailcoat

Designer: Paul Tazewell

Designed by renowned costume designer Paul Tazewell, the wardrobe of the original broadway cast aimed to combine historically accurate pieces with contemporary features that reflected the modernized storytelling of the musical itself. Ensuring movement while maintaining shape and silhouette was achieved through fabrics such as suede and silk taffeta.

Harold Gray, James Whitcomb RileyThomas Meehan & Martin Charnin - Annie

Annie - Iconic Red Dress

Designer: Susan Hilferty

Made by the theatrical household name Susan Hilferty, Annie’s iconic red dress is a key icon of theatre history, marking a signature look for the character and highlighting a pivotal point of change and growth in her story. Creating a simplistic yet memorable dress through a polyester and nylon blend, the dress ensures the expected comfort and simplicity of a child’s clothing while showing the growth of her wardrobe and what she is able to access in her newfound position.

L. Frank Baum, Paul Tietjens, & Victor Flemming - The Wizard Of Oz

Dorothy Gale - Blue Gingham Dress & Red Ruby Slippers

Designer: Adrian Greenburg

Originally dating back to the illustrations of the book by L. Frank Baum, Dorothy Gale’s famous blue and white gingham dress has remained a staple character piece for generations, accompanied by her famed ruby red shoes and tan brown wicker basket. Designed for the 1939 film by Adrian Greenburg, the ensemble in its entirety defined the golden age of Hollywood with its ability to remain vibrant through technicolour film and balancing elements of youth with popular styles and silhouettes of the time.

L. Frank Baum, Gregory Maguire, Stephen Schwartz - Wicked

Glinda Upland & Elphaba Throppe - 'For Good' Blue Gown & Black Dress

Designer: Susan Hilferty

Straying away from the heavy use of pinks and bright greens from the 2024 and 2025 films, the costume design for the original 2003 broadway production of Wicked leaned into purples and blues in contrast to darker greens and blacks. Originally designed by Susan Hilferty, Glinda and Elphaba’s wardrobe took a subtler approach to ornamentation and silhouette as opposed to the films, while still heavily reflecting the paths taken by these leads as the story progresses. Elphaba grows into sleekness and ability to blend into darkness with small reinforcements of colour and life, while Glinda spreads out into bubble-shapes clothing and soft pastel colours that portray her newfound status.

Andrew Lloyd Webber - Evita

Eva Perón - White Dior Act II Dress

Designer: Christopher Oram

Created and designed originally by Christopher Oram, the most notable piece from the musical chronicling the life of Argentine political leader Eva Perón is the cream, sleeveless, diamond encrusted dress, inspired by the one worn by the real Eva Perón gifted to her by Christian Dior. The look highlights a pivotal change in the direction of Perón’s life as she begins her days as first lady of Argentina, signalling a new status of class and power while struggling to grip onto her roots.

T.S. Elliot & Andrew Lloyd Webber - Cats

All Cats - Fur Suits

Designer: John Napier

The revolutionary blend of human and feline features originated from costume designer John Napier. In addition to the initial spectacle of having cat and human hybrid creations, the idea was to create a sense of familiarity and relatability for audience members when seeing human elements to the characters onstage. The design choice is a part of the production’s unique approach to storytelling, creating a fantastical setting to allow audiences’ full immersion.

Alan Jay Lerner & Frederick Loewe - My Fair Lady

Eliza Doolittle - Black & White Horse Race Dress

Designer: Cecil Beaton

A production lucky enough to tell the tale of the transformation of Eliza DoLittle through timeless stars Dame Julie Andrews on the broadway stage and Audrey Hepburn on the big screen, My Fair Lady has stood the test of time for a musical classic in both song and in looks. Original broadway production costume designer Cecil Beaton reinterpreted looks from the Black Ascot of 1910 to create the famous black and white dress and hat from the horse racing sequence of both the show and the film. Balancing Eliza’s experimentation of newfound class with her roots of extravagance and bold patterns, the look perfectly blends her two worlds colliding and how she continues to balance them moving forward in the story.
